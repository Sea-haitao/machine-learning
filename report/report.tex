\begin{abstract}
  We analyse the 4{,}424-row Student Dropout dataset, predicting the
  three-class academic outcome (Dropout / Enrolled / Graduate) from 36
  demographic, academic, and macroeconomic features. A standardised
  preprocessing pipeline, t-SNE visualisation, three clustering
  algorithms, two baseline classifiers, and a validation-driven
  improvement pass constitute the mandatory tasks. Our best baseline
  model is Logistic Regression with test accuracy $0.764$ and
  macro-F1 $0.696$; tuning lifts the Decision Tree's macro AUC from
  $0.73$ to $0.84$. The \emph{Enrolled} class is the hardest across
  every model, consistent with the t-SNE overlap it exhibits with
  the \emph{Graduate} cluster. In the open-ended exploration,
  Random Forest becomes the strongest predictive model
  (test macro-F1 $0.714$, macro AUC $0.889$), while engineered
  academic, family, and economic features plus RFE improve
  cross-validated Random Forest macro-F1 from $0.680$ to $0.695$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% Aim: ~0.5 page. Highlight findings and main results up front.

The Student Dropout dataset \cite{uci_dropout} contains 4{,}424
undergraduate records with 36 features covering academic path,
demographics, socio-economic context, and first/second-semester
curricular performance. Our task is to predict the final academic
outcome: Dropout, Enrolled (still enrolled at observation time), or
Graduate. The class distribution is imbalanced
(Graduate $50\%$, Dropout $32\%$, Enrolled $18\%$), which shapes
both our metric choices (macro-F1, macro AUC) and our model
regularisation decisions (\texttt{class\_weight="balanced"}).

Headline results:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Logistic Regression (baseline) reaches test accuracy $0.764$
        and macro-F1 $0.696$, outperforming an unconstrained Decision
        Tree that overfits to $1.000$ train accuracy but only $0.688$
        on test.
  \item t-SNE shows two well-separated clusters (Dropout,
        Graduate) with Enrolled students dispersed between them —
        consistent with confusion-matrix evidence that Enrolled is
        the hardest class.
  \item $K{=}3$ is the preferred cluster count for K-means,
        Hierarchical, and GMM. K-means wins on internal metrics;
        GMM wins on external agreement with true labels; all external
        scores are moderate (Purity $\approx 0.65$).
  \item Validation-driven tuning lifts the Decision Tree's macro AUC
        from $0.729$ to $0.838$, with the binding constraints being
        \texttt{max\_depth=5} and \texttt{class\_weight="balanced"}.
  \item A broader Phase 6 search over seven model families selects
        Random Forest as the strongest final model (test macro-F1
        $0.714$, macro AUC $0.889$), and leak-free feature engineering
        with RFE gives a modest but consistent CV macro-F1 gain.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mandatory Tasks}

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}
% Aim: ~0.5 page. Methods + observations.

The raw file is semicolon-delimited with no missing values in any of
the 36 columns. We classify features as continuous (19 columns,
e.g.\ \emph{Age at enrollment}, \emph{Unemployment rate}), ordinal
categorical, and nominal categorical (e.g.\ \emph{Marital status},
\emph{Nationality}, \emph{Mother's occupation}). The preprocessing
pipeline:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Strip stray whitespace/quotes from column headers and rename
        the misspelled \emph{Nacionality} (as shipped in the raw file)
        to \emph{Nationality}.
  \item One-hot encode nominal categoricals via
        \texttt{pd.get\_dummies(drop\_first=True)}, expanding $36$
        raw features to $238$ engineered columns.
  \item Standardise the $19$ continuous columns with
        \texttt{StandardScaler} (zero mean, unit variance).
  \item Label-encode the target (Dropout $\to 0$, Enrolled $\to 1$,
        Graduate $\to 2$).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Observation:} no explicit missing values, but several nominal
columns (\emph{Mother's qualification}, \emph{Nationality}) have
long-tailed distributions where specific codes occur fewer than $10$
times. After the $70/30$ stratified split we observe $14$
zero-variance one-hot columns in the training fold; they are
retained (\texttt{StandardScaler} handles them safely) to preserve
the schema across splits.

\subsection{Data Visualisation (t-SNE)}
% Aim: ~0.5 page with figure.

We apply t-SNE (\texttt{sklearn.manifold.TSNE}, perplexity $= 30$,
\texttt{max\_iter} $= 1000$, \texttt{random\_state} $= 42$) to the
preprocessed matrix.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{tsne_2d.png}
  \caption{2D t-SNE projection coloured by true class. Dropout
    (red) and Graduate (green) occupy largely separate regions of the
    embedding; Enrolled (orange) is dispersed, overlapping both.}
  \label{fig:tsne2d}
\end{figure}

We ran t-SNE at perplexities $p \in \{5, 15, 30, 50\}$ to assess
sensitivity to the local-neighbourhood bandwidth (see
\texttt{outputs/tsne\_perplexity\_comparison.png}). At $p=5$ the
embedding fragments each class into fine sub-clusters --- likely
sub-types of dropouts (e.g.\ financially stressed vs.\ academically
struggling) --- but exhibits no global separation. From $p=15$
upward a clear Dropout/Graduate axis emerges and remains stable
through $p=50$, indicating the separation is intrinsic to the
feature space rather than an artefact of one bandwidth choice.
Enrolled students consistently lie \emph{between} the two main
groups at every perplexity, which previews the per-class F1
difficulty later observed in Phases 4--5.

\subsection{Clustering Analysis}
% Aim: ~1 page. Algorithm descriptions + comparison table + figure.

We compare three clustering algorithms: K-means (centroid-based with 
Euclidean distance), agglomerative hierarchical clustering (bottom-up 
merging with Ward linkage), and Gaussian Mixture Models (probabilistic, full 
covariance). All approaches are implemented with a fixed cluster number 
$K=3$. Cluster quality is assessed via three internal validation metrics 
(Silhouette, Calinski--Harabasz, Davies--Bouldin) and three external 
metrics (ARI, NMI, Purity) against the true class labels.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Clustering comparison at $K{=}3$. Internal metrics favour
    K-means; external metrics favour GMM; all external scores are
    moderate.}
  \label{tab:cluster}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrrrrr}
    \toprule
    Algorithm    & Silhouette & CH      & DB    & ARI   & NMI   & Purity \\
    \midrule
    K-means      & $0.247$    & $731.45$& $1.73$& $0.162$ & $0.166$ & $0.645$ \\
    Hierarchical & $0.212$    & $645.82$& $1.76$& $0.123$ & $0.134$ & $0.624$ \\
    GMM          & $0.138$    & $472.38$& $2.98$& $0.194$ & $0.166$ & $0.649$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Determination of the Clustering Method:} At $K=3$, a consistent performance 
ranking is observed: K-means achieves the best overall performance, followed by 
hierarchical clustering, with GMM being the weakest. This ordering is mainly attributed 
to the data characteristics after one-hot encoding: the dataset becomes sparse, 
high-dimensional and dominated by binary features. K-means relies on Euclidean distance 
to identify compact cluster centroids. In such a sparse binary feature space, samples from the same latent category 
share a large number of activated features, making centroid representations meaningful 
and clustering effective. Hierarchical clustering is also capable of capturing inherent 
group structures, yet its fixed Ward linkage criterion limits the global optimization of 
within-cluster compactness, leading to slightly inferior performance. By contrast, GMM 
assumes each cluster follows an elliptical Gaussian distribution, which is strongly violated 
by the sparse, axis-aligned binary features in this study. Consequently, the Expectation-Maximization 
algorithm consequently fails to derive stable covariance matrices, resulting in negative 
Silhouette scores (indicating poor cluster separation) and considerably lower external matching scores.

Accordingly, we select \textbf{K-means with $K=3$ as the primary clustering approach, with hierarchical 
clustering serving as a supplementary reference.} GMM is not recommended due to its unsatisfactory 
performance and inherent incompatibility with the data structure.

In addition, all methods show only moderate external evaluation scores, suggesting that unsupervised 
clustering partitions can only partially align with real academic outcome labels. Such moderate agreement 
is common in educational datasets, as student academic performance is driven by multidimensional factors 
that cannot be fully captured by distance-based clustering alone. This finding is consistent with the 
t-SNE visualization, where the “Enrolled” category exhibits no clear separability in low-dimensional space.

\subsection{Prediction: Training and Testing}
% Aim: ~1 page. Models + split + confusion matrices + comparison.

\paragraph{Target and models.} We predict the three-class
\texttt{Target} column. Two simple model classes are selected:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Decision Tree} (\texttt{DecisionTreeClassifier},
        \texttt{random\_state}$=42$) --- interpretable, handles mixed
        feature types natively, no scaling required.
  \item \textbf{Logistic Regression}
        (\texttt{LogisticRegression}, \texttt{max\_iter}$=1000$) ---
        standard linear baseline for multi-class classification.
        Fitted on \texttt{StandardScaler}-transformed features
        (fit on the training fold only) to avoid numerical overflow
        on the unscaled $238$-column design matrix.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Split and protocol.} Stratified $70/30$ split with
\texttt{random\_state}$=42$: 3{,}096 training / 1{,}328 test samples.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Enrolled is the hardest class for both
models (per-class F1 $\approx 0.37$ for DT, $0.46$ for LR). This
matches the t-SNE observation: currently-enrolled students occupy an
overlapping region between Dropout and Graduate. LR outperforms the
Decision Tree on every balanced metric; the Decision Tree's
$1.000$ training accuracy with only $0.688$ on test is a textbook
overfitting signal that we address via pruning and class-weighting
in Section~\ref{sec:eval}.

\subsection{Evaluation and Choice of Prediction Model}
\label{sec:eval}

\paragraph{Metrics.} We compute accuracy, precision, recall, and F1
(macro and weighted) from the test confusion matrices, and OvR
multiclass ROC/AUC using predicted probabilities.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Baseline test-set metrics. LR dominates on every
    class-balanced criterion.}
  \label{tab:metrics}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
    \toprule

    \bottomrule

\paragraph{Validation-driven improvement.} We compare two strategies
on the training split only:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CV (GridSearchCV)} with 5-fold
        \texttt{StratifiedKFold(shuffle=True, random\_state=42)},
        scoring \texttt{f1\_macro}.
  \item \textbf{Holdout validation}: $80/20$ split of the training
        fold; hyperparameters chosen on the validation fold; final
        model refit on the full training fold.
\end{itemize}

Both strategies prefer Decision Tree \texttt{max\_depth=5} with
\texttt{class\_weight="balanced"}, and Logistic Regression
$C{=}1.0$ with \texttt{class\_weight="balanced"}. Tuning lifts the
Decision Tree's macro AUC from $0.729$ to $0.838$ --- the biggest
improvement in the project, directly addressing the overfitting
observed in Section~2.4. LR gains are marginal because the baseline
is already well-regularised.

\paragraph{Further discussion (100--200 words).}
On the fixed 70/30 test split, Logistic Regression outperforms the
Decision Tree on class-balanced metrics. Baseline LR reaches
macro-F1 $\approx 0.70$ and macro AUC $\approx 0.86$, while the
unconstrained DT sits at macro-F1 $\approx 0.62$ and macro AUC
$\approx 0.73$ --- consistent with higher variance and overfitting
risk in the tree. ROC/AUC analysis confirms the ordering, and the
\emph{Enrolled} class has the lowest one-vs-rest AUC across every
model, matching the confusion-matrix pattern where Enrolled is most
often predicted as Graduate. We improved both models via
validation without ever touching the test set. CV and holdout
tuning select similar LR settings and test performance barely
changes, suggesting LR is already near a good optimum under this
feature representation. For the Decision Tree, tuning (depth cap
plus class weighting) lifts macro AUC from $0.73$ to $0.84$ and
narrows the gap to LR. We select the CV-tuned LR as the final
operating model on macro-F1/AUC held-out performance.

\section{Open-ended Exploration}

The open-ended component extends the mandatory DT/LR baseline in two
directions: (i) broader hyperparameter tuning across additional model
families, and (ii) domain-driven feature engineering with feature
selection. All model selection uses stratified cross-validation on the
training split; final comparisons are reported on the same fixed
70/30 test split used in Phases 4--5.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning and Model Comparison}

Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, SVC, MLP, and Random Forest with

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Selection}

The second exploration constructs twelve business-driven features
from the cleaned raw feature table before model fitting. These features
summarise academic performance (\emph{semester pass rates},
\emph{total pass rate}, \emph{grade mean}, \emph{grade trend},
\emph{high failure risk}), family background (\emph{average parental
qualification}, \emph{same parent occupation}), and economic pressure
(\emph{financial stress}, \emph{tuition/debt status}, scholarship, and
macroeconomic risk). The clearest single engineered signal is
\emph{high failure risk}: students with pass rate below $50\%$ have a
Dropout rate of $0.876$, versus $0.180$ for the remaining students.

To avoid leakage, feature construction and Recursive Feature
Elimination (RFE) are placed inside the cross-validation pipeline. The
feature space changes from 36 raw features to 48 after engineering,
then RFE selects 30 features for the Random Forest estimator.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption{Feature-engineering validation using 5-fold stratified CV
    with Random Forest. The gain is modest, but it is measured inside
    the CV pipeline and therefore does not rely on full-data feature
    selection.}
  \label{tab:phase6-features}
  \begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
    \toprule
    Feature Set & Dim. & Accuracy & F1\,\textsubscript{macro}
                & F1\,\textsubscript{weighted} \\
    \midrule
    Original raw features & $36$ & $0.767$ & $0.680$ & $0.749$ \\
    Engineered + RFE      & $30$ & $0.773$ & $0.695$ & $0.760$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The result is not a large jump, but it is credible: the engineered
features improve macro-F1 by $1.54$ percentage points while reducing
dimensionality. This is stronger evidence than a large but leaky gain
would be. Polynomial or power transformations were deliberately
disabled after skewness inspection, because they add feature noise and
make the business interpretation weaker without solving the main
Enrolled-class overlap.

\section{Conclusion}
% Aim: ~0.5 page.

We built an end-to-end supervised learning pipeline for a $4{,}424$-sample,
three-class student-outcome problem. The Enrolled class is inherently
harder than Dropout and Graduate --- a finding consistent across
t-SNE visualisation, clustering analysis, and every classifier we
trained. Logistic Regression with standardised features is the best
mandatory-task baseline, but the open-ended model search shows that a
tuned Random Forest is the stronger final predictive model
(test macro-F1 $\approx 0.714$, macro AUC $\approx 0.889$).
Feature engineering plus RFE gives a smaller, complementary gain,
improving CV macro-F1 from $0.680$ to $0.695$ while reducing the
feature set to 30 selected predictors. Practically, the persistent
Enrolled prediction gap suggests the dataset would benefit from
time-to-event framing (survival analysis) rather than static
three-class classification --- a direction we flag for future work.
